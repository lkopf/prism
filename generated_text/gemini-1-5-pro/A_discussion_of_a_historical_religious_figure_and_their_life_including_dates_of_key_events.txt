The life of Gautama Buddha, born Siddhartha Gautama in Lumbini, Nepal, circa 563 BCE, unfolded as a profound journey from princely opulence to ascetic renunciation and finally to enlightenment and the establishment of Buddhism, marked by his renunciation of palace life around 29 years old in approximately 534 BCE, a period of intense spiritual seeking and ascetic practices under various teachers, during which he explored extreme forms of self-mortification before realizing the futility of such extremes, culminating in his attainment of enlightenment, traditionally dated to 528 BCE while meditating beneath a Bodhi tree in Bodh Gaya, India, after which he became known as the Buddha, or the "Awakened One," and began teaching the Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, and the concept of dependent origination, spreading his teachings throughout the Ganges Valley for the next 45 years, attracting a diverse following of monks, nuns, and lay practitioners drawn to his message of liberation from suffering, with key moments including his first sermon at Deer Park in Sarnath, the establishment of the sangha, or monastic community, and the gradual development of a complex body of scriptures and philosophical interpretations of his teachings, continuing his tireless travels and teachings until his death, or parinirvana, around 483 BCE at Kushinagar, leaving behind a legacy that continues to influence millions across the globe through various schools and traditions that have evolved over the centuries, each interpreting and applying his teachings in unique ways, but ultimately striving towards the same goal of enlightenment and the cessation of suffering.
Prophet Muhammad, born in Mecca, Arabia, in 570 CE, experienced a transformative journey from orphanhood to becoming the founder of Islam, marked by his profound spiritual experiences, including the Night of Power in 610 CE when he received the first revelation from God through the Angel Gabriel in the Hira cave, laying the foundation for the Quran and his prophetic mission, subsequently facing persecution in Mecca due to his monotheistic teachings that challenged the polytheistic beliefs prevalent at the time, leading to the Hijra, or migration, to Medina in 622 CE, a pivotal moment marking the establishment of the first Muslim community and the beginning of the Islamic calendar, where he established a social and political order based on Islamic principles, leading to conflicts with the Meccans, culminating in the Battle of Badr in 624 CE, a significant victory for the Muslims, and eventually, the peaceful conquest of Mecca in 630 CE, after which he cleansed the Kaaba of idols and established Islam as the dominant religion in the region, continuing to receive revelations and consolidate the teachings of Islam until his death in 632 CE in Medina, leaving behind a legacy that has profoundly shaped the religious, political, and cultural landscape of the world through the Quran and his teachings, which continue to inspire and guide over a billion Muslims globally.
Jesus of Nazareth, whose birth is traditionally dated around 4 BCE in Bethlehem, Judea, lived a life marked by teachings of love, compassion, and forgiveness, beginning his public ministry around the age of 30, performing miracles, healing the sick, and preaching the coming of the Kingdom of God, attracting large crowds with his teachings that emphasized humility, charity, and spiritual transformation, including the Sermon on the Mount where he articulated key principles of his ethical teachings, such as the Beatitudes and the Golden Rule, gradually gaining followers while also facing opposition from religious authorities who viewed his teachings as a threat to their established power and traditions, culminating in his arrest, trial, and crucifixion in Jerusalem around 30-33 CE under the Roman governor Pontius Pilate, an event central to Christian belief, followed by his resurrection, a cornerstone of Christian faith, which led to the spread of Christianity through the efforts of his apostles, particularly Paul the Apostle, who played a key role in establishing Christian communities throughout the Roman Empire, resulting in the development of a complex theological and philosophical tradition based on his life and teachings, influencing billions of people across the globe for centuries and continuing to inspire various interpretations and expressions of Christian faith.
Confucius, born Kong Qiu in 551 BCE in the state of Lu during the Spring and Autumn period of ancient China, embarked on a life dedicated to scholarship, teaching, and social reform, serving as a minor government official while developing his philosophical system based on ethical principles, including ren (benevolence), yi (righteousness), li (ritual propriety), and zhi (wisdom), aiming to restore social harmony and moral order through education and virtuous governance, traveling throughout various states in China for over a decade, seeking to persuade rulers to adopt his principles but facing numerous setbacks and disappointments as his ideas were often rejected by those in power, dedicating his later years to compiling and editing classic texts, including the Five Classics, which became foundational to Chinese culture and education, and continuing to teach and inspire a growing circle of disciples until his death in 479 BCE, leaving behind a legacy that has profoundly shaped Chinese thought, culture, and governance for over two millennia, influencing not only China but also other East Asian countries, with his teachings continuing to be studied and debated by scholars and practitioners worldwide.
Moses, a central figure in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, whose life is shrouded in both historical and religious narrative, likely lived during the 13th century BCE, leading the Israelites out of slavery in Egypt, a momentous event known as the Exodus, receiving the Ten Commandments from God on Mount Sinai, forming the cornerstone of Jewish law and morality, leading the Israelites through the desert for forty years, during which he faced numerous challenges and rebellions from the Israelites as they struggled to maintain their faith and commitment to God, communicating with God through the burning bush and receiving divine guidance for the construction of the Tabernacle, a portable sanctuary where God's presence resided among the Israelites, delivering numerous laws and teachings to the Israelites, shaping their religious and social identity, never entering the Promised Land himself but appointing Joshua as his successor to lead the Israelites into Canaan, leaving behind a legacy as a prophet, lawgiver, and leader who shaped the religious and cultural identity of the Jewish people, his story continuing to inspire and guide generations through its powerful themes of liberation, covenant, and divine revelation.

Abraham, considered the patriarch of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, lived approximately between the 20th and 18th centuries BCE, embarking on a journey of faith from his birthplace in Ur of the Chaldees to the land of Canaan, responding to God's call to leave his homeland and travel to a new land that God promised to his descendants, entering into a covenant with God, marked by the promise of land, descendants, and blessings, facing trials and challenges, including the near-sacrifice of his son Isaac, demonstrating his unwavering faith and obedience to God's will, fathering Ishmael through Hagar and Isaac through his wife Sarah, these sons becoming the progenitors of the Arab and Jewish peoples respectively, establishing the practice of circumcision as a sign of the covenant with God, ultimately settling in Hebron where he purchased a burial cave for his wife Sarah, establishing a lineage that would become central to the Abrahamic faiths, his story embodying themes of faith, obedience, and covenant, continuing to inspire and resonate with millions of believers across the globe.
Zoroaster, a prophet and religious reformer who likely lived in ancient Persia between the 10th and 6th centuries BCE, founded Zoroastrianism, a monotheistic religion centered on the worship of Ahura Mazda, the supreme god of wisdom and light, challenging the traditional polytheistic beliefs prevalent in ancient Persia, preaching a dualistic cosmology of good versus evil, represented by Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu, the destructive spirit, emphasizing ethical principles such as good thoughts, good words, and good deeds, introducing the concept of free will and individual responsibility in choosing between good and evil, facing opposition and persecution from adherents of the traditional religions, eventually gaining royal patronage under the Achaemenid dynasty, which helped to spread Zoroastrianism throughout the Persian Empire, contributing to the development of key religious concepts, including the concept of heaven and hell, judgment after death, and the eventual triumph of good over evil, his teachings profoundly influencing subsequent religions, including Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, and continuing to be practiced by a small community of Zoroastrians today, primarily in India and Iran.
 Guru Nanak, born in 1469 CE in the village of Rai Bhoi di Talwandi, now known as Nankana Sahib, in present-day Pakistan, founded Sikhism, a monotheistic religion emphasizing the unity of God and the importance of devotion and selfless service, undertaking extensive travels throughout India and beyond, engaging in interfaith dialogues with Hindus, Muslims, and other religious figures, promoting a message of universal brotherhood and equality, rejecting the caste system and other social inequalities prevalent in Indian society at the time, establishing the concept of langar, a free community kitchen open to all regardless of caste, creed, or social status, composing numerous hymns and poems that formed the basis of the Guru Granth Sahib, the Sikh scripture, appointing a successor to continue his mission, establishing a lineage of ten gurus who further developed and consolidated Sikh teachings and traditions, passing away in 1539 CE in Kartarpur, leaving behind a legacy of spiritual and social reform that continues to inspire millions of Sikhs worldwide.
Mahavira, born Vardhamana Mahavira around 599 BCE in Kshatriyakund, India, became the 24th and last Tirthankara of Jainism, renouncing his princely life at the age of 30 to pursue spiritual liberation, practicing extreme asceticism for twelve years, including fasting, meditation, and nudity, enduring immense physical and mental hardships to overcome all attachments and karmic bonds, attaining Kevala Jnana, or perfect knowledge and enlightenment, after which he became known as Mahavira, meaning "great hero," teaching the principles of Jainism, including ahimsa (non-violence), satya (truthfulness), asteya (non-stealing), brahmacharya (celibacy), and aparigraha (non-attachment), emphasizing the importance of respecting all living beings and minimizing harm to all creatures, advocating for a life of simplicity and detachment from material possessions, traveling throughout India for over 30 years, spreading his teachings and gaining followers from all social classes, organizing the Jain community into a four-fold order of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen, achieving moksha, or liberation from the cycle of birth and death, at the age of 72 in 527 BCE in Pava, India, leaving behind a legacy of non-violence, compassion, and spiritual self-discipline that continues to influence the lives of millions of Jains around the world.
 Baha'u'llah, born Mirza Husayn-Ali in 1817 in Tehran, Persia, founded the Baha'i Faith, a religion emphasizing the oneness of God, the unity of humanity, and the progressive revelation of God's will through a series of divine messengers, becoming a follower of the Bab, a religious leader who proclaimed himself to be a herald of a new divine messenger, imprisoned and exiled for his beliefs, first to Baghdad, then to Constantinople and Adrianople, and finally to the penal colony of Acre in Ottoman Palestine, during which time he declared himself to be the promised messenger foretold by the Bab, revealing numerous writings and teachings that outlined the principles of the Baha'i Faith, promoting the establishment of a world federation, the elimination of all forms of prejudice, universal education, and the harmony of science and religion, attracting followers from various religious backgrounds, his teachings emphasizing the essential unity of all religions and the need for humanity to embrace a global perspective, facing continued persecution and imprisonment until his death in 1892 near Acre, his resting place becoming the holiest site for Baha'is, his writings and teachings forming the basis of the Baha'i Faith, which continues to spread globally, promoting peace, unity, and social justice.
