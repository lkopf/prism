Roosevelt, urging caution and advocating for responsible use of nuclear technology, a testament to his deep commitment to humanity and his unwavering belief in the power of science for the betterment of mankind, as evidenced by his numerous publications, lectures, and public appearances dedicated to promoting scientific understanding and fostering a more peaceful and just world, leaving an enduring legacy as one of the greatest scientific minds of all time, inspiring countless individuals to pursue scientific inquiry and strive for a deeper understanding of the universe and our place within it, while simultaneously reminding us of the ethical responsibilities that come with scientific advancements and the importance of using knowledge wisely for the benefit of all.

Marie Curie, a pioneering physicist and chemist, and the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, awarded in Physics in 1903 and later in Chemistry in 1911, dedicated her life to scientific research, particularly in the field of radioactivity, conducting groundbreaking studies at the University of Paris, France, where she became the first female professor in 1906, and along with her husband, Pierre Curie, discovered two new elements, polonium and radium, revolutionizing the understanding of atomic structure and paving the way for advancements in medicine and other fields, despite facing significant challenges as a woman in a male-dominated scientific community, persevering with unwavering determination and making extraordinary contributions to science, which ultimately led to her becoming a role model for aspiring scientists around the world, especially for women pursuing careers in science, and demonstrating that dedication, hard work, and a passion for knowledge can overcome any obstacle, while also highlighting the importance of supporting women in STEM fields and ensuring equal opportunities for all, regardless of gender or background, leaving behind an enduring legacy of scientific excellence and inspiring generations of scientists to follow in her footsteps.

Nelson Mandela, anti-apartheid revolutionary, political leader, and philanthropist, who served as President of South Africa from 1994 to 1999, spent 27 years imprisoned on Robben Island and other prisons for his activism against apartheid, emerging as a symbol of hope and resilience for millions around the globe, and upon his release in 1990, played a crucial role in negotiating the end of apartheid and establishing a democratic South Africa, earning him the Nobel Peace Prize in 1993 for his extraordinary contributions to peace and reconciliation, demonstrating exceptional leadership and unwavering commitment to justice and equality, inspiring people worldwide to fight for their rights and stand against oppression, and leaving a lasting legacy as a champion of human rights and a beacon of hope for a more just and equitable world, reminding us of the power of forgiveness, reconciliation, and the unwavering pursuit of justice, even in the face of seemingly insurmountable obstacles.

Mahatma Gandhi, leader of India's nonviolent independence movement against British rule, born in Porbandar, India, in 1869, employed principles of satyagraha, or nonviolent resistance, to mobilize millions of Indians in their struggle for freedom, leading peaceful protests, boycotts, and civil disobedience campaigns that ultimately contributed to India's independence in 1947, becoming an iconic figure of peace and social justice, inspiring movements for civil rights and nonviolent resistance around the world, demonstrating the power of nonviolent action to achieve social and political change, and leaving a legacy of peace, tolerance, and nonviolent resistance that continues to inspire activists and social change movements globally, reminding us of the power of peaceful resistance in the face of oppression and injustice.


Queen Elizabeth II, the longest-reigning monarch in British history, serving as Queen of the United Kingdom and other Commonwealth realms from 1952 until her death in 2022, witnessed significant social, political, and technological transformations during her reign, providing a sense of continuity and stability during times of change, and fulfilling her duties with unwavering dedication and grace, earning the respect and admiration of people worldwide, demonstrating exceptional leadership and a deep sense of duty throughout her long and remarkable life, becoming a symbol of national unity and a respected figure on the global stage, while also navigating numerous challenges and controversies with poise and resilience, leaving behind a legacy of service, dedication, and unwavering commitment to her role as monarch.

William Shakespeare, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's greatest dramatist, born in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, in 1564, penned numerous iconic plays, including Hamlet, Romeo and Juliet, Macbeth, and King Lear, exploring universal themes of love, loss, ambition, and betrayal, enriching the English language with countless phrases and expressions that are still in use today, and leaving an enduring legacy on literature, theatre, and the English language, inspiring countless writers, actors, and artists for centuries, and continuing to be studied and performed around the world, solidifying his place as a literary giant whose works transcend time and culture.

Abraham Lincoln, the 16th President of the United States, serving from 1861 until his assassination in 1865, led the country through the tumultuous years of the American Civil War, issuing the Emancipation Proclamation in 1863, which declared the freedom of enslaved people in Confederate-held territory, playing a pivotal role in the abolition of slavery in the United States, and delivering the Gettysburg Address, one of the most famous and enduring speeches in American history, reaffirming the principles of equality and democracy, leaving a lasting legacy as a champion of human rights and a symbol of national unity, while also navigating the complexities of wartime leadership and facing immense political pressure, ultimately guiding the nation through its greatest crisis and shaping the course of American history.
